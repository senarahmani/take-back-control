If your own content was copied & spread

You don't have to chase every copy. There's a shorter path.

When content you made is copied and spread without your consent, it feels endless — find one copy, two more appear. This plan closes off how people find it, registers it so platforms auto-block new copies, and points you to the real tools. All free.

The checklist and log below stay on your device. Nothing here is uploaded or sent anywhere.
Three things that change everything

Why it feels hopeless — and why it isn't

The panic comes from picturing the job as "delete every copy." That version really is endless. These shifts make it finite.

01 The dandelion

Chasing each copy is like picking up every seed a dandelion scatters — impossible. Instead: cut off the wind (search engines spread the seeds) and register the fingerprint once so big platforms auto-block new copies. After that, a stray copy on an obscure site is a seed on concrete — it exists, but can't spread and almost nobody finds it.

02 The water mains

Removing it from one search engine then spotting it on another isn't failure. Engines are wildly unequal in traffic. Shutting the big main (Google) cuts most of the flow even while a trickle comes through a side pipe. The goal isn't zero results everywhere — it's closing every main that carries real traffic, which is a short list.

03 The files don't matter

You do not need the original files to protect yourself. The two highest-impact steps — search removal and infrastructure reports — work from a web address and a name. Having the files unlocks one bonus tool (StopNCII), but losing them doesn't lose the plan.

The plan, in order

Six steps, start to finish

Roughly in this order. Stop and come back anytime — the checklist remembers where you are.

1

Register a "fingerprint" with StopNCII

Needs the person depicted~20 minOnly if you have the files

StopNCII is the free, nonprofit tool closest to "make this disappear" — and it never sees your images. On your own device, you select the files; your device turns each into a code (a "hash") and sends only the code. The image never leaves your device.

Partners then auto-detect and block copies — Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, Reddit, Snap, PornHub, OnlyFans, plus Google & Bing search. Save the case number and PIN; they aren't recoverable. No files? Skip to step 2 — it doesn't need them.

2

Close the search engines

Needs the person depicted~30 minHighest impact

The single most powerful step, and it works from a name and URLs — no files. Close the "mains" in order:

  • Google — use the dedicated explicit-imagery removal flow, not the generic one. It tries to catch duplicates too.
  • Bing — Microsoft's non-consensual imagery report. This also largely covers Yahoo and DuckDuckGo, which use Bing's index — one request, three engines.
  • Yandex and Brave Search — each has its own removal request, to catch what the others won't.

Once Google and Bing are done, you've covered how nearly anyone would find this.

3

Report it at the source platform

Needs the account holder~15 min

If the leak traces to an account you still control, report through it so the request is tied to a verified identity — platforms act faster on those. State plainly: you are the person depicted, the content was shared without your consent, and you request removal under their policy and the Take It Down Act. Include the URLs. Keep the confirmation.

About closing that account: you can close it whenever you like. Keeping a dormant account open does not give leakers any "incentive to stop" — there's no deal; the leak already happened. The only reason to keep it open briefly is that it's your cleanest identity anchor for this one report. File the report, then close it on your own timeline.
4

Report the site's infrastructure

A helper can do this~10 min per site

For a stubborn or overseas site, don't fight the site — go to the companies underneath it. You can't evict a tenant who won't answer, but you can call the landlord and the utility company.

  • Look up who's behind the URL with a "who is hosting this" tool — find the CDN (often Cloudflare), the host, and the registrar.
  • Report to Cloudflare if it's behind them. They forward to the real host and can reveal who that is — your next target.
  • Report to the host and registrar directly, citing non-consensual imagery and the Take It Down Act.

5

Send a Take It Down Act notice

A helper can draft & send~10 minLast reviewed June 2026

Since May 19, 2026, US-covered platforms must remove reported non-consensual intimate images — including those originally shared consensually but redistributed without permission — within 48 hours, or face FTC penalties. A valid notice needs four things: a signature, the content's location (URLs), a brief statement that it's non-consensual, and contact info. Template below. Keep every notice; a platform that blows the deadline becomes evidence for an FTC complaint.

    Honest answers

    Questions people actually ask

    Does this work if the content was originally posted consensually?›

    Yes. Consenting to create or share something once is not consent to have it redistributed elsewhere. Content first shared on a creator platform and then leaked is treated as non-consensual where it was re-posted without permission.

    One wrinkle: Google's search-removal form asks that you're not currently being paid to commercialize that same content. If there's no active, earning account, this doesn't apply. If there is, the Take It Down notice and infrastructure reports don't carry that condition, and a copyright (DMCA) removal may also apply. A support org can help pick the right lever.

    Should I close the account the leak came from?›

    You can, on your own timeline. A dormant account doesn't give leakers any reason to stop — there's no negotiation happening, and the leak already occurred. The only reason to keep it open briefly is as a verified-identity anchor for the source-platform report. File that report, then close it whenever you're ready.

    What if we don't have the original files?›

    You still do almost everything. Search removal, infrastructure reports, source reports, and Take It Down notices all work from a URL and a name. Only StopNCII needs files, and it's a bonus. Even a copy downloaded from a leak site can be fingerprinted.

    I removed it from one search engine but found it on others. Did it fail?›

    No — that's expected. Removal is per-engine. Closing Google and Bing covers the overwhelming majority of how anyone finds content (Bing also feeds Yahoo and DuckDuckGo). The goal is the engines that carry real traffic, not literal zero everywhere.

    The site is overseas and ignores everything. What now?›

    You usually don't need it deleted at the source — you need it unfindable. Deindex it from search, and report the US/EU infrastructure underneath it (CDN, host, registrar), which often follows the rules even when the site won't. The hotline is excellent at this part.
