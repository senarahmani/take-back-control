When someone you knew shared them

What they did is a crime — and you have real options.

When a partner, ex, or acquaintance posts or sends intimate images of you without consent, two things are true at once: the images can be removed, and the person who did it may have broken the law. This guide covers both — getting it down, and protecting yourself — at a pace you control.

The checklist and log below stay on your device. Nothing here is uploaded or sent anywhere.
First, your safety. If you feel physically unsafe, or the person is threatening you, that comes before any takedown. Contact your local emergency number if you're in danger, and consider the National Domestic Violence Hotline (1-800-799-7233) for safety planning. A protective/restraining order is also an option a local advocate or attorney can help with. The images will still be removable after you're safe.
Before you do anything else

Two things that protect your case

01 Preserve, don't delete

It's a natural urge to make it vanish — but first screenshot and save the evidence: the post, the URL, the username, the date, any messages where they admitted it or threatened you. This is what makes a police report or protective order stick. Save it somewhere only you can reach. Then start removals.

02 Don't confront them

Confronting or threatening the person feels powerful, but it can tip them off to delete evidence, escalate the situation, or muddy a future case. Let the systems and, if you choose, the police do the pressure. Your job is to document and report — not to negotiate with someone who already crossed this line.

The plan, in order

Getting it down

The removal steps are the same proven sequence. Because the person is known to you, a criminal report is also on the table — that's step 4.

1

Register a "fingerprint" with StopNCII

Needs the person depicted~20 min

If you have the images (or can screenshot them), StopNCII fingerprints them on your own device — the image never leaves it — and partners auto-block copies across Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, Reddit, Snap, and search. Save the case number and PIN.

2

Report to the platform it's on

You or a helper~15 min

Every major platform now has a non-consensual intimate imagery report path and must act within 48 hours under the Take It Down Act. Report the specific post/account. State you're the person depicted and didn't consent. Keep the confirmation. The notice template is below.

3

Close the search engines

Needs the person depicted~30 minHighest impact

Removing it from search is what stops people from finding it. Works from your name and the URLs — no files needed.

  • Google — dedicated explicit-imagery removal flow (catches duplicates).
  • Bing — also covers Yahoo and DuckDuckGo.
  • Yandex and Brave — their own forms.
Google removal help
4

Consider a police report

Your choice, fullycriminal option

Because the person is identifiable, this may be a prosecutable crime. Most US states have non-consensual image laws, and the federal Take It Down Act makes knowingly sharing such images a crime. Reporting is entirely your decision — some survivors do, some don't, and both are valid. If you want to, bring your preserved evidence to local police, and ask CCRI or a local advocate to help you navigate it. They can also help you weigh a civil suit or protective order.

You don't have to decide now. Preserving evidence (step 1 of the section above) keeps the door open without committing you to anything.
5

Report stubborn sites' infrastructure

A helper can do this~10 min per site

If it spread to a site that won't comply, report the companies underneath it — the CDN (often Cloudflare), the host, the registrar — citing non-consensual imagery and the Take It Down Act.

    Honest answers

    Questions people ask

    Do I have to report it to the police?›

    No. It's entirely your decision, and removal works whether or not you involve law enforcement. Some survivors report and some don't — both are valid. Preserving evidence now simply keeps the option open in case you change your mind later.

    I sent the images willingly. Does that mean I have no case?›

    No. Sharing an intimate image with one person in a relationship is not consent for them to post or distribute it. The law treats the non-consensual distribution as the wrong, regardless of how the image was originally created.

    Should I message them to take it down?›

    Generally no. Contact can tip them off to delete evidence, escalate, or complicate a case. Let the platforms, search engines, and (if you choose) the police apply the pressure. If there's any safety concern, route everything through an advocate.
